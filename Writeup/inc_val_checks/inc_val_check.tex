No Variety Specification:
\begin{equation*}
  U_{ijt} = \beta_iX_{ijt} + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{equation*}

In this specification, love of variety does not enter. Only preference for characteristics. We see that $\approx95\%$ of the probability mass is on products 4, 5, and 6, with an inclusive value of 11.36. All that happened with the introduction of this product is mass shifted from 5 $\rightarrow$ 6. Product 6 $=$ product 5 in value, thus it makes sense that it appears probability is being taken from 5. 

Variety Specification:
\begin{equation*}
  U_{ijt} = \beta_iX_{ijt} + \gamma_i\log(1+\Sigma^2) + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{equation*}

where $\Sigma^2 = X_{jt} - \frac{1}{s}\sum_{s=1}^{t-1}X_{js}$ and $\gamma_i\in\{6, 9, 12\}$, we find that higher levels of $\gamma$ lead to higher inclusive values. Interestingly, the addition of a product leads to stable choices of that product across the three variety levels, with lower $\gamma$ more likely to select it due to its value being the same as 5's. IV decreases for all variety lovers. Increases in base model. Decreases are slight, though.

As the new product's quality changes, we see different trends. At $X_6=1.0$, the inclusive value changes are very close to at $X_6=5.0$. This is understandable, as we have fluctuation between $X_1$ and $X_5$. But, when $X_6=3$, we see greater losses in inclusive value as no one picks $X_3$ anyways. Only the low $\gamma$ type picks the new entrant at $0.21\%$.

Adding something completely new raises inclusive value both at introduction and in the long run. For the "redundant" products, inclusive value increases initially for all $\gamma$ types, but then decreases in the long run.
